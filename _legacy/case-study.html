Mission Compose — a planning workspace that collapses six tools into one.

A single multiplayer canvas for joint mission planning — with AI checks running alongside the team, not in front of them.

Client
Royal Navy · Joint Forces Command
Year
2024 — 2025
Discipline
UX · AI · Service Design
Team
11 across 14 months
Status
● Live · 1,200 weekly users

Joint planning used to mean six tools, three classifications, and a whiteboard nobody could photograph. We rebuilt it as a single multiplayer canvas — with AI checks running alongside the team, not in front of them.

The brief

Joint planners were spending more time reconciling tools than planning. Six systems, each with its own model of the world; reviews stalled at the boundaries.

We were asked to design a workspace that could hold a plan end-to-end — and that wouldn't crumble under classified, multi-organisation collaboration.

Sixteen weeks embedded with three planning cells

We watched 142 planning sessions, recorded the friction in real time, and came back with a model of how a plan actually moves through the building — versus how the org chart suggested it should.

  • 142 observed sessions across 3 cells.
  • 38 hours of structured operator interviews.
  • 9 cross-org workshops, including 2 wargames.
"It's the first tool that moves at the speed we actually plan."— Cdr. K., Joint Planning Cell, after 6 weeks of pilot use

A canvas for plans, not a database with chrome

We replaced six form-driven tools with a single spatial canvas — courses of action laid out side-by-side, AI-assisted ROE and route checks running in the margin, every change attributable.

Critically: the AI never overwrites. It annotates. The human stays the planner.

Adopted, adapted, expanding

Within nine months Mission Compose was the default planning surface in three commands. The numbers below are from the 6-month post-rollout review.
