Designing trust into autonomous mission systems.

When the model becomes a teammate, the seam between human judgement and machine inference becomes a design surface — and the most important one we have.

Author
Maya Okafor
With
Jonas Lindqvist
Published
28 April 2026
Read time
12 minutes
Topic
AI · UX

For decades, defence software was built around a single mental model: the operator was the decision-maker, and the system was a faster pen. That model is breaking down. The next generation of mission systems contains models that can recommend, summarise, even act — and the question of where the human ends and the system begins is no longer rhetorical.

We've been embedded inside three programmes wrestling with this. What follows is what we've learned about designing the seam — the place where human judgement meets machine inference — and why we think trust isn't a feeling to be earned, but a property to be built.

Trust is a structural property, not a vibe

The instinct, when adoption is low, is to add a friendly explanation. A tooltip. A "why am I seeing this?" link. These help, marginally. But what actually moves the needle is something more architectural: making the model's uncertainty visible by default, and making the operator's override irreversible by design.

When operators can see, at a glance, where the system is confident and where it is guessing — and when their corrections are demonstrably re-shaping the model's next answer — trust accumulates as a side-effect of normal work. You don't have to ask for it.

"The model is a teammate. The interface is the contract."— Field embed, 14th week

Six shifts we keep designing for

  • From answer to argument. Stop showing one recommendation; show the two or three the model considered, and why one won.
  • From confidence as a number to confidence as a shape. A 78% certainty bar is meaningless. A bounded region on a map is operationally readable.
  • From override to negotiation. Let the operator constrain the model ("never route through this airspace") rather than just reject its output.
  • From log to ledger. Every model action should leave a citable, replayable trace — for after-action review and for legal accountability.
  • From dashboard to dialogue. The most-used surface is increasingly conversational. Design it like a workflow, not a chatbot.
  • From single-shot to standing query. Mission users want models that watch on their behalf, not models that answer when prompted.

Where we're taking this next

Over the next two quarters we're publishing a pattern library covering all six shifts, with reference implementations in our open-source token system. If you're working on adjacent problems — particularly in the human-on-the-loop space — we'd like to compare notes.

The mission is too important for our software to feel like a black box. The good news is, none of this requires new science. It requires a design discipline that takes operator agency as a first-class concern, and is willing to ship the boring infrastructure to support it.
